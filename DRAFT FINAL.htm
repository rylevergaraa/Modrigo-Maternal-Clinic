Maternity FAQs

Evidence-based answers for expectant mothers and nursing staff at Modrigo Maternity Clinic.

This reference guide covers common questions about pregnancy, prenatal care, warning signs, labor, and postpartum care. Always consult your attending physician for advice specific to your condition. For emergencies, call 555-0199 ext. 24 immediately.
🤰 General Pregnancy
Your Estimated Due Date (EDD) is calculated using Naegele's Rule — 280 days (40 weeks) from the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP). An early ultrasound (ideally before 12 weeks) can confirm or adjust this date based on the baby's measurements.
📅 Normal pregnancy: 38–42 weeks
  • 1st Trimester (Weeks 1–13): Rapid fetal development; nausea, fatigue, and breast tenderness are common.
  • 2nd Trimester (Weeks 14–27): Energy usually returns; baby movements (quickening) begin around weeks 18–22.
  • 3rd Trimester (Weeks 28–40+): Baby gains weight quickly; back pain, swelling, and Braxton Hicks contractions increase near delivery.
  • G – Gravida: Total number of pregnancies (including current)
  • T – Term: Deliveries at 37+ weeks
  • P – Preterm: Deliveries before 37 weeks
  • A – Abortions: Spontaneous or elective losses
  • L – Living: Children currently alive
  • M – Multiple: Twin or higher-order pregnancies
Example: G3 T2 P0 A1 L2 M0
Recommended weight gain depends on your pre-pregnancy BMI:
  • Underweight (BMI <18.5): 12.5–18 kg
  • Normal weight (18.5–24.9): 11.5–16 kg
  • Overweight (25–29.9): 7–11.5 kg
  • Obese (≥30): 5–9 kg
Most weight gain should occur in the 2nd and 3rd trimesters. Your nurse will monitor this at every visit.
⚠️ Note: Gaining too much or too little weight can affect your baby's development. Never go on a diet during pregnancy without medical supervision.
Travel is generally safe during the 2nd trimester (weeks 14–27). Avoid long-distance travel after 36 weeks. During travel:
  • Wear your seatbelt below the bump, across the lap and shoulder
  • Take breaks every 1–2 hours on road trips to walk and stretch
  • Stay hydrated and avoid sitting still for long periods (DVT risk)
  • Bring copies of your prenatal records and your doctor's contact info
Best time to travel: 2nd trimester (weeks 14–27)
Yes, sex is generally safe throughout a normal pregnancy. The baby is protected by the amniotic sac and the strong uterine muscles. Some positions may become uncomfortable as the pregnancy progresses — side-lying or woman-on-top positions are often more comfortable in later pregnancy.
⚠️ Avoid sex if: your water has broken, you have placenta previa, you experience unexplained bleeding or cramping, or your doctor has specifically advised against it.
First-time mothers typically feel baby movements (quickening) between weeks 18–22. Mothers who have been pregnant before may notice movement as early as 16 weeks. Movements feel like flutters, bubbles, or gentle taps at first, then stronger kicks and rolls as the baby grows.
⚠️ After 28 weeks: You should feel at least 10 movements within 2 hours. Use the Kick Counter in your Patient Portal to track this. Contact the clinic immediately if movements significantly decrease.
🥗 Nutrition & Lifestyle
  • Folic acid (400–800 mcg/day): Start before conception. Prevents neural tube defects.
  • Iron (27 mg/day): Prevents anemia; supports increased blood volume.
  • Calcium (1,000 mg/day): Bone and teeth development for baby.
  • Vitamin D (600 IU/day): Works with calcium; supports immune function.
  • DHA/Omega-3: Brain and eye development.
  • Iodine: Thyroid function and fetal brain development.
A complete prenatal multivitamin prescribed at your first visit covers most of these.
  • 🐟 High-mercury fish: Shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish
  • 🥩 Raw or undercooked meat, poultry, seafood, eggs (risk of Salmonella, Listeria, Toxoplasma)
  • 🧀 Unpasteurized dairy and soft cheeses: Brie, Camembert, Feta, blue cheese
  • 🌱 Unwashed raw produce
  • ☕ Excess caffeine: Limit to under 200 mg/day (about 1 cup of coffee)
  • 🍷 Alcohol: No safe amount during pregnancy — avoid completely
  • 🌿 Herbal teas and supplements without physician approval — some are unsafe in pregnancy
Yes — for most women, 150 minutes of moderate exercise per week is safe and beneficial. Recommended activities: walking, swimming, prenatal yoga, and stationary cycling.

Benefits include: reduced back pain, better sleep, improved mood, lower risk of gestational diabetes, and easier labor.

Avoid: Contact sports, activities with fall risk, hot yoga, and lying flat on your back after the 1st trimester.
⚠️ Stop exercise and consult the clinic if: chest pain, dizziness, severe shortness of breath, vaginal bleeding, contractions, or fluid leaking occur during activity.
Many common medications are unsafe during pregnancy. Always check with your doctor or nurse before taking any medication — including pain relievers, antacids, antihistamines, and herbal products.

Generally considered safe (confirm with your doctor): Paracetamol/acetaminophen for pain and fever.

Avoid: Ibuprofen (NSAIDs), especially in the 3rd trimester; aspirin in high doses; most decongestants in the 1st trimester.
⚠️ Never self-medicate during pregnancy. Even "natural" products like herbal supplements can be harmful.
🩺 Prenatal Care & Monitoring
For low-risk pregnancies:
  • Weeks 4–28: Monthly (every 4 weeks)
  • Weeks 28–36: Every 2 weeks
  • Week 36 to delivery: Weekly
High-risk pregnancies (preeclampsia, diabetes, twins, etc.) require more frequent monitoring as directed by your obstetrician.
⚠️ Never skip prenatal visits — many serious complications like preeclampsia and gestational diabetes are silent and only caught through routine checks.
FHR monitoring tracks the baby's heartbeat to assess well-being. It is done via handheld Doppler during clinic visits, or electronically (CTG/EFM) during labor.

A normal baseline FHR is 110–160 bpm. Patterns outside this range — especially late decelerations or prolonged bradycardia — may signal fetal distress and require immediate clinical action.Normal FHR: 110–160 bpm
Tests vary by trimester:
  • All trimesters: BP, weight, urine dipstick, fundal height, FHR
  • 1st trimester: Blood type, Rh factor, CBC, hepatitis B, HIV, syphilis, urine culture
  • 2nd trimester (18–20 wks): Anatomy ultrasound; anomaly scan
  • 24–28 weeks: OGTT for gestational diabetes (GDM screening)
  • ~36 weeks: Group B Streptococcus (GBS) swab; fetal presentation check
The NST monitors the baby's heart rate in response to its own movements. A "reactive" result (two or more accelerations in 20 minutes) indicates good fetal well-being.

NSTs are typically ordered for: post-term pregnancies, gestational diabetes, hypertension, decreased fetal movement, IUGR (growth restriction), or any high-risk condition. The test is non-invasive and painless — just two monitors placed on the abdomen for 20–40 minutes.
⚠️ Warning Signs & Complications
Go to the clinic or hospital immediately for:
  • 🩸 Vaginal bleeding at any stage of pregnancy
  • 🤕 Severe or persistent headache that won't go away
  • 👁️ Blurred vision, seeing spots, or sudden vision changes
  • 🦵 Sudden severe swelling of face, hands, or feet
  • 🌡️ Fever above 38°C (100.4°F)
  • 🤰 Significantly reduced or absent fetal movement after 28 weeks
  • 💧 Fluid leaking or gushing from the vagina (possible ruptured membranes)
  • 🔴 Severe abdominal pain or cramping
  • 🫁 Difficulty breathing or chest pain
🚨 Do not wait — call 555-0199 ext. 24 or go to the nearest emergency room immediately.
Preeclampsia is diagnosed when BP reaches ≥140/90 mmHg after 20 weeks, combined with protein in the urine or signs of organ damage. Symptoms: severe headache, visual disturbances, upper abdominal pain, and rapid swelling.

Management includes:
  • Close BP and urine monitoring
  • Antihypertensive medications as prescribed
  • Magnesium sulfate to prevent seizures (eclampsia)
  • Possible early delivery depending on gestational age and severity
⚠️ Nursing note: BP ≥160/110 requires immediate physician notification. Preeclampsia can progress to eclampsia (seizures) very rapidly.
GDM is high blood sugar first diagnosed during pregnancy. It is screened at 24–28 weeks using an Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT). If diagnosed:
  • Diet modification (low glycemic index foods, controlled carbohydrates)
  • Blood glucose self-monitoring (fasting and postprandial)
  • Physical activity as tolerated
  • Insulin therapy if dietary management is insufficient
  • Increased fetal surveillance (NST, biophysical profile, growth scans)
GDM usually resolves after delivery, but significantly increases the lifetime risk of Type 2 diabetes for the mother.Screening: OGTT at 24–28 weeks
Placenta previa occurs when the placenta partially or completely covers the cervical opening. It can cause painless vaginal bleeding, especially in the 3rd trimester.

Management: Pelvic rest (no sex, no vaginal exams), bed rest as directed, and scheduled cesarean delivery if the placenta is still covering the cervix at term. Many cases of low-lying placenta in early pregnancy resolve as the uterus grows.
⚠️ Any painless vaginal bleeding should be reported to the clinic immediately — it may indicate placenta previa or placental abruption.
Placental abruption is the premature separation of the placenta from the uterine wall before delivery. It can be a medical emergency. Signs include: sudden severe abdominal pain, a rigid abdomen, and vaginal bleeding (though bleeding may be hidden internally).

Risk factors include high blood pressure, trauma to the abdomen, cocaine use, smoking, and previous abruption. It requires immediate emergency care.
🚨 If you experience sudden severe abdominal pain with or without bleeding, go to the emergency room immediately.
Miscarriage (spontaneous abortion) occurs in approximately 10–20% of known pregnancies, most commonly in the 1st trimester. The most common cause is chromosomal abnormality in the embryo — it is rarely caused by anything the mother did or did not do.

Signs include vaginal bleeding, cramping, and loss of pregnancy symptoms. Not all bleeding means miscarriage — many pregnancies continue normally after early bleeding. Report any vaginal bleeding to the clinic promptly.
⚠️ If you experience heavy bleeding with clots and severe cramping, seek emergency care.
🏥 Labor & Delivery
  • 🔵 Lightening: Baby drops lower into the pelvis; you may breathe easier but feel more pelvic pressure
  • 🩸 Bloody show: Pink or blood-tinged mucus discharge as the cervical plug releases
  • 💧 Rupture of membranes: Water breaking — a gush or steady trickle of clear amniotic fluid
  • 🔄 Regular contractions: Tightenings that come at consistent intervals, growing stronger, longer, and closer together
Come to the clinic at 5-1-1: contractions every 5 minutes, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour

  • Stage 1 — Early Labor: Cervix dilates from 0–6 cm. Contractions are mild to moderate, 5–30 minutes apart. You can usually stay home during this stage.
  • Stage 1 — Active Labor: Cervix dilates 6–10 cm. Contractions are stronger, 3–5 minutes apart, lasting 45–60 seconds. Go to the clinic.
  • Stage 1 — Transition: The final push to 10 cm. Most intense contractions. Short but powerful.
  • Stage 2 — Pushing & Birth: From full dilation to delivery of the baby. Lasts minutes to a few hours.
  • Stage 3 — Delivery of Placenta: Placenta delivered 5–30 minutes after the baby.
An episiotomy is a surgical incision made in the perineum (tissue between vagina and anus) to widen the vaginal opening during delivery. It used to be done routinely but is now only performed when medically necessary — such as when the baby needs to be delivered quickly, or when a severe tear seems inevitable.

The incision is repaired with stitches after delivery. Healing typically takes 2–4 weeks. Keep the area clean, use a peri bottle with warm water, and avoid straining at stool.
Non-medical options:
  • Lamaze breathing techniques and relaxation
  • Warm water (shower or bath) — helps relax muscles
  • Position changes, massage, heat packs
  • Walking and movement during early labor
Medical options:
  • Epidural: Most effective — local anesthetic injected into the epidural space of the spine. Numbs from waist down.
  • IV pain medications: Opioids like morphine or pethidine — reduce pain but do not eliminate it
  • Nitrous oxide (laughing gas): Inhaled, takes the edge off pain without full numbing
Discuss your birth plan and pain preferences with your doctor before labor begins.
👶 Postpartum & Newborn Care
The postpartum period covers approximately 6 weeks after delivery:
  • The uterus contracts back to its pre-pregnancy size (involution)
  • Lochia (vaginal discharge): bright red for 3–4 days → pinkish-brown → yellowish-white by 2 weeks
  • "Baby blues" — mood swings, tearfulness — are normal for up to 2 weeks due to hormone shifts
  • Breastfeeding is established; nipple soreness is common in the first weeks
  • Perineal healing (especially after episiotomy or tears) takes 2–6 weeks
⚠️ Postpartum Depression (PPD): If sadness, hopelessness, inability to bond, or intrusive thoughts persist beyond 2 weeks — or are severe at any point — tell your care team. PPD affects 1 in 7 mothers and is treatable.
For baby:
  • Complete, perfectly tailored nutrition for every stage
  • Maternal antibodies protect against infection — especially important in the first 6 months
  • Lower risk of SIDS, ear infections, respiratory illness, and childhood obesity
  • Better long-term cognitive and immune development
For mother:
  • Stimulates uterine contractions (helps prevent postpartum hemorrhage)
  • Burns ~300–500 extra calories per day
  • Reduces long-term risk of breast cancer, ovarian cancer, and type 2 diabetes
  • Strengthens emotional bonding through oxytocin release
WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months
Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is excessive bleeding after delivery — more than 500 mL after vaginal birth or 1,000 mL after C-section. Signs include:
  • Soaking more than one pad per hour after the first few days home
  • Passing large clots (larger than a golf ball)
  • Bright red bleeding that increases rather than decreases over time
  • Dizziness, rapid heartbeat, or feeling faint
🚨 This is a medical emergency. Return to the clinic or nearest ER immediately.
Most healthcare providers recommend waiting at least 6 weeks before resuming sex — enough time for the cervix to close, lochia to stop, and any tears or episiotomy repairs to heal. Your doctor will assess this at your 6-week postpartum checkup.

Note: You can become pregnant again very quickly after delivery (even before your first period returns), particularly if you are not exclusively breastfeeding. Discuss contraception with your doctor at your postpartum visit.
